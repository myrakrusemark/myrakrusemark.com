The Seeds Were Planted Long Ago

The current moment didn't emerge from nowhere. It's the culmination of decades of economic, social, and political shifts that left millions feeling abandoned.

1979-1981
The Neoliberal Turn
Economic Shift

Margaret Thatcher (1979) and Ronald Reagan (1981) usher in a new economic orthodoxy: privatization, deregulation, tax cuts for the wealthy, and the weakening of unions. The neoliberal era begins.

This wasn't just policy—it was ideology. The belief that markets solve everything would reshape the world for 40 years.

1989-1991
"The End of History"
Triumphalism

The Berlin Wall falls. The Soviet Union collapses. Francis Fukuyama declares liberal democracy has won forever. Western elites assume globalization will inevitably spread prosperity and freedom everywhere.

This hubris would prove costly. The assumption that liberal institutions would continue spreading has now "collapsed," and autocratic systems have become the global norm.

1993-2000
Globalization Accelerates
TradeJobs

NAFTA passes. China joins the WTO. Manufacturing jobs begin leaving the American heartland. From 1969-1993, the poorest 5% of Americans saw income fall 34% while the richest 5% saw income rise 43%.

Both parties embrace "free trade" while communities hollow out. The seeds of Trump's coalition are planted in Rust Belt towns watching factories close.

September 11, 2001
The War on Terror Begins
Turning Point

9/11 reshapes American politics. The Patriot Act expands surveillance. The invasions of Afghanistan and Iraq begin what would become America's longest wars. Trust in institutions begins its long decline.

The "forever wars" would eventually fuel both left and right populism—and Trump's "America First" isolationism.

2008
The Great Financial Crisis
Crisis Point

The global economy nearly collapses. Banks are bailed out; homeowners are not. Millions lose homes, jobs, and savings. Many are still reeling from this crisis when Trump arrives.

This is the moment when faith in the system breaks for many. The Tea Party rises on the right; Occupy Wall Street on the left. Both are symptoms of the same disease.

2010-2015
Austerity & Social Media
PolicyTechnology

European austerity devastates Greece, Spain, Portugal. The IMF admits: "Instead of delivering growth, some neoliberal policies have increased inequality."

Meanwhile, Facebook and Twitter become primary news sources. Algorithmic feeds reward outrage. Filter bubbles form. The information ecosystem fragments.

2015-2016
The Populist Breakthrough
Turning Point

Trump descends the escalator. Brexit shocks the world. Harvard research finds support explained "as a reaction against cultural changes that threaten the worldview of once-predominant sectors."

The establishment is blindsided. They'd ignored the brewing discontent for too long.

2020
COVID-19: The Accelerant
PandemicPolarization

The pandemic doesn't create new divisions—it accelerates existing ones. Lockdown debates become culture war. Trust in institutions (CDC, media, government) craters further.

Economic disruption hits working class hardest. Remote workers are fine; service workers lose everything. The class divide widens into a chasm.

2021-2023
Inflation & Institutional Failure
Economy

Post-pandemic inflation hits hard. Mortgage payments nearly double. Voters feel economic pain despite "good" GDP numbers. Democrats tout statistics while people struggle at grocery stores.

The disconnect between elite economic metrics and lived experience becomes politically fatal.

2024
Trump Returns: The Broligarchy Rises
Present Day

Trump wins with a "multiracial, multi-ethnic coalition of grievance populism." Tech billionaires (Musk, Thiel, others) align openly with the movement.

The "broligarchy"—a fusion of tech wealth, masculine grievance culture, and anti-establishment posturing—becomes a political force. Silicon Valley, once libertarian-leaning but culturally liberal, pivots hard right.

2025
The Multipolar Moment
World Order

The Munich Security Conference 2025 declares the theme "multipolarization"—acknowledging the end of American unipolarity.

China and India rise. BRICS expands. The US turns inward. Scholars warn Trump's victory will "embolden populist movements and authoritarian regimes globally."

Did Spanish Flu Cause WWII?

Not directly—but pandemics destabilize societies in ways that enable extremism.

The Spanish Flu (1918-1920) killed more people than WWI itself. It:

  • Disrupted economies already stressed by war
  • Created distrust in governments that downplayed or mishandled the crisis
  • Left psychological trauma and social dislocation
  • Hit working-age adults hardest, disrupting families and labor

The pandemic didn't cause fascism, but it contributed to the fragile, traumatized societies in which fascism could take root. Similarly, COVID didn't make us crazy—it revealed and accelerated fractures that already existed.

How Did Democracies Recover Last Time?

The post-WWII recovery offers hard-won lessons.

Research shows democracies survived the 1930s when they had:

  • Longer democratic traditions — Countries accumulated "democratic capital" over generations
  • United middle classes — Where liberals co-opted workers with reforms, extremism struggled
  • Strong social democratic coalitions — Nordic countries survived by building broad coalitions
  • Economic recovery — Prosperity undermines extremist appeal

After WWII, fascism was discredited by its association with genocide and military defeat. The Marshall Plan rebuilt economies. European integration created shared institutions. Sustained democratic practice accumulated new "democratic capital."

What Can Americans Look Forward To?

The Honest Reality

Things will likely get worse before they get better. Democratic decline is in its 19th year globally. The forces driving this—inequality, cultural anxiety, institutional distrust—aren't going away quickly.

But History Offers Hope

Democracies have recovered before. It required discrediting extremism, rebuilding trust through competent governance, and addressing the real economic grievances that fueled populism. None of this is impossible.

The Multipolar Future

The US will no longer be the sole superpower. This means less global influence but potentially less "imperial overstretch." Americans may need to focus more on domestic renewal—which might not be bad.

What Actually Helps

Research suggests: economic security, functioning institutions, cross-cutting social ties, and time. The pendulum has swung before. The question is how much damage occurs before it swings back.

"Countries with longer histories of democracy seem to have been largely immune from fascism, and the link between economic growth and extremist voting behavior was correspondingly weaker."

— Cambridge Economic History Research

The Bottom Line

We're not in unprecedented territory—but we are in dangerous territory.

The combination of economic anxiety, cultural backlash, institutional decay, and technological disruption has created conditions similar to the 1930s. COVID accelerated these trends but didn't create them. They were 40 years in the making.

Recovery is possible but not guaranteed. It requires addressing real grievances, rebuilding trust, and—crucially—time. The American experiment has survived crises before. Whether it survives this one depends on choices made now.
